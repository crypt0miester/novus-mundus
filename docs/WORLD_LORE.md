# Novus Mundus — World Lore & Story line

> *"The old world is gone. What rises from its bones is ours to shape — or to lose."*

---

## I. The World Before

Before the Sundering, there was **Aeondral** — a vast, interconnected civilization spanning every continent. Not one empire, but many: rival kingdoms, trading republics, temple states, and warlord domains. They warred. They traded. They built wonders. For millennia, they thrived.

But Aeondral was built on something it did not understand.

Beneath the bedrock, threaded through the roots of mountains and the floors of oceans, ran veins of a crystallized energy the ancients called **Novis** — the raw essence of the world itself. They mined it. Forged weapons from it. Burned it for power. Built empires on it. Novis fueled everything: their economies, their armies, their grandest ambitions.

They never asked where it came from. They never asked what would happen when they took too much.

---

## II. The Sundering

No one alive can say exactly what triggered it.

The histories — what little survived — speak of a tremor that began deep beneath the Korthain Mountains and did not stop. For seven days the earth groaned. On the eighth, the world split open.

**The Sundering** was not a war. It was not a plague. It was the world breaking apart.

The great continental plates fractured. Entire coastlines sank beneath boiling seas. Mountain ranges collapsed into chasms miles deep. Cities that had stood for a thousand years vanished in minutes — swallowed whole, as if the earth simply closed its mouth.

The sky turned black with ash for a generation. Seas rose, then receded, leaving new shorelines no map could recognize. The world that emerged was unrecognizable — a shattered mosaic of the one that came before.

Most of Aeondral's billions perished. Their cities became tombs. Their roads became ravines. Their knowledge was buried under miles of rubble and reshaped stone.

But not everything was lost.

---

## III. The Reborn World — Novus Mundus

What the survivors call **Novus Mundus** is not a new world. It is the corpse of the old one, rearranged.

New landmasses rose from the ocean floor — some volcanic and raw, others ancient seabeds pushed skyward, studded with the ruins of drowned cities. Continents that were once connected now sit separated by new seas. Lands that were once coastal now tower as highland plateaus.

The geography is alien, but the bones of the old world are everywhere. Dig deep enough in any settlement and you'll find worked stone, shattered pottery, corroded metal — the detritus of a civilization that thought it would last forever.

**This is where you are. This is what you've inherited.**

---

## IV. The Player — Who You Are

You are no one important.

You are not a chosen hero. You were not blessed by gods or marked by prophecy. You are a **survivor** — one of the countless thousands scattered across Novus Mundus, scraping together a life from the wreckage of someone else's civilization.

Maybe you were born in a makeshift settlement. Maybe you wandered for years before finding a place worth defending. It doesn't matter. What matters is that you're here now, and you've decided to stop surviving and start **building**.

You've claimed a patch of land. You've driven stakes into the dirt. You've hired your first workers, armed your first guards. You are, for the first time, not just a survivor — you are a **lord of nothing**, with everything to gain.

The world does not care about your ambitions. Other survivors have the same idea. The land is crawling with creatures that will eat you if given the chance. And somewhere, deep below, the veins of Novis still pulse — dimmer than before, but not dead.

**Rise, or be forgotten. The world has no room for the timid.**

---

## V. The Kingdoms

Not all of Novus Mundus emerged at once.

The Sundering was not a single event but a cascading collapse that played out over years. Some regions stabilized quickly — the bedrock held, the land settled, survivors gathered. These became the **First Kingdoms**: the earliest territories where civilization began to reform.

Other regions took longer. Volcanic activity subsided slowly. Seas drained from new basins over decades. Tectonic plates ground into new positions. Even now, new landmasses continue to surface — territories that were submerged or unstable finally becoming habitable.

**Each kingdom is a territory that has become viable for settlement.** Some are old and established, with entrenched powers and deep-rooted Houses. Others are raw and new, where the ground is still warm and opportunity is everywhere — if you're bold enough to seize it.

### Why New Kingdoms Appear

The world is still healing. The Sundering fractured the continental plates into hundreds of fragments. As the deep geological forces settle, new fragments rise, cool, and stabilize. Each new kingdom represents a territory that has finally become livable.

This means:

- **Older kingdoms** are more developed but more competitive. The best land is claimed. The strongest Houses are entrenched. Breaking in is hard.
- **New kingdoms** are wild and open. Everyone starts equal. The first to organize, the first to build alliances — they shape everything that follows.

### Per-Kingdom Ages

Each kingdom progresses through its own narrative arc as it matures:

**Age of Ashes** — *The beginning.* The land is raw. Ruins outnumber settlements. Survivors arrive and stake claims. No one has power yet. Everything is contested. Encounters are frequent — the wild forces haven't been pushed back. This is the most dangerous and most exciting time.

**Age of Crowns** — *Houses rise.* Power consolidates. Lords become rulers. Alliances form and fracture. Castles are built. Territory is claimed and defended. The political game begins in earnest. Resources become strategic. Wars are fought not for survival, but for dominance.

**Age of Dominion** — *The kingdom matures.* Great Houses control vast territories. The economy is sophisticated. Trade routes are established. The wild forces are pushed to the margins. Power is about influence, alliances, and cunning — not just military strength. The question shifts from "who will survive?" to "who will rule?"

---

## VI. The Wild Forces

Novus Mundus is not empty.

Long before the first human civilization rose, the world teemed with creatures — apex predators adapted to a planet of extreme geology and volatile energy. The ancients of Aeondral pushed them to the margins, into the deep forests, the mountain heights, the ocean trenches. Civilization was a wall between humanity and the wild.

The Sundering tore that wall down.

With cities collapsed and armies gone, the wild forces reclaimed the world. They are not evil. They are not cursed. They are **the natural order of a planet that never belonged to us.** We borrowed the surface for a few millennia. Now we're back to competing for it.

### The Bestiary

The creatures of Novus Mundus exist on a spectrum of danger:

| Tier | Creature | Nature |
|------|----------|--------|
| **Common** | **Goblin Raiders** | Scavengers. Small, cunning, and territorial. They pick through ruins and attack anyone who wanders too close to their claimed salvage. Dangerous in numbers. |
| **Uncommon** | **Troll Warlords** | Apex territorial predators. Massive, regenerating, and fiercely intelligent. They claim entire valleys and mountainsides. Not mindless — they remember grudges. |
| **Rare** | **Wyverns** | Aerial hunters. Nesting in cliff faces and ruined towers, they patrol vast hunting grounds. A wyvern attack is fast, brutal, and often comes from above without warning. |
| **Epic** | **Dragons** | Ancient. The oldest predate Aeondral itself. They are not animals — they are forces of nature with intelligence to match. A dragon does not attack; it *decides*. |
| **Legendary** | **Ancient Wyrms** | The deep ones. Creatures of the earth itself, burrowed into the world's crust for eons. The Sundering disturbed their sleep. When one surfaces, the land around it trembles for miles. Some say they feed on Novis. |

These creatures are not spawned by darkness or summoned by magic. They are **wildlife** — the original inhabitants of this world. Humanity is the invasive species. Every settlement carved from the wilderness is territory taken from something that was there first.

Clearing encounters isn't heroism. It's survival. It's expansion. It's the fundamental act of civilization: pushing back the wild to make room for the human.

## VII. The Relic Economy

Everything you own was someone else's first.

The currency of Novus Mundus is **salvage**. The old world's ruins are an almost inexhaustible source of materials — if you're willing to dig for them and fight for the right to keep them.

### Resources

- **Cash** — Not coins minted by a current authority. It's a mixture of old-world currency, precious metals pried from ruins, and trade tokens that settlements have agreed to honor. It works because everyone agrees it works. For now.

- **Weapons** — Reforged from the armories and foundries buried in the rubble. Ancient metallurgy was superior to anything survivors can produce from scratch. The best weapons are the oldest ones — pulled from sealed vaults or pried from the hands of the dead. Your smiths don't create; they **restore**.

- **Produce** — Food grown in the ash-enriched soil of Novus Mundus. The Sundering devastated the surface, but centuries of volcanic fallout made the new topsoil absurdly fertile. Farming is viable, but every field must be defended from both raiders and wildlife.

- **Drays** — Low-sided carts dragged by beasts of burden or salvaged engines. Aeondral had sophisticated transport networks; their remnants — wheels, axles, harness systems, even primitive motors — are stripped, rebuilt, and yoked to whatever still pulls. A column of drays at dawn is what an army looks like when it actually moves.

### NOVI — Fragments of Power

Deep in the ruins, in the collapsed mines and shattered vaults of Aeondral, survivors occasionally find intact crystals of **Novis** — the same energy the old world built itself on and ultimately destroyed itself with.

These crystals, called **NOVI** in common trade, are the most valuable substance in Novus Mundus. They are:

- **Finite**: The veins are depleted. What remains is what was already mined and stored by the ancients, now scattered through the ruins.
- **Powerful**: A single NOVI crystal contains enough energy to fuel a forge for a month, power ancient machinery, or enhance weapons beyond what conventional smithing allows.
- **Dangerous**: The old world consumed too much Novis. Every crystal spent is a question: are we making the same mistake?

**Locked NOVI** is Novis that has been bonded to a location or purpose — embedded in a building's foundation, fused into a weapon, channeled into research. Once locked, it cannot be extracted without destroying what it powers. It is spent. Gone. Part of the infrastructure now.

**Reserved NOVI** is Novis held in its raw crystal form — portable, tradeable, withdrawable. It is wealth, but also potential. The tension between spending NOVI to grow and hoarding it as a safety net is the central economic dilemma of Novus Mundus.

## VIII. The Houses

In the chaos after the Sundering, the strong gathered the weak. Protection was traded for loyalty. Loyalty became obligation. Obligation became hierarchy.

**Houses** are the political units of Novus Mundus — families, factions, or alliances bound by oath, ambition, or necessity. A House is not a guild. It is a **claim to legitimacy**. It says: we are not just survivors. We are a power. We have a name, a sigil, and the strength to defend both.

### How Houses Form

Any lord with enough influence can declare a House. But declaration is cheap — **survival is the test.** A House that cannot defend its members, that cannot hold territory, that cannot reward loyalty, will dissolve as quickly as it formed. The world is littered with the banners of Houses that lasted a single season.

The Houses that endure share common traits:

- **A seat of power** — an estate, a castle, a fortified settlement. Without land, a House is just a name.
- **Members who benefit** — reinforcements in battle, shared intelligence, economic cooperation. A House must be worth belonging to.
- **Enemies worth having** — a House that threatens no one controls nothing. Power is measured by who opposes you.

### House Politics

This is where the game truly lives.

Alliances between Houses are strategic, not sentimental. They form when interests align and shatter when they don't. A House that was your ally this season may be your rival the next — not because of betrayal, but because the map changed, because a new kingdom opened, because a resource vein was discovered in contested territory.

**Trust is a resource more scarce than NOVI.**

The greatest lords of Novus Mundus are not the ones with the largest armies. They are the ones who know when to fight, when to negotiate, and when to let two rivals exhaust each other before sweeping in to claim what's left.

## IX. The Estates

Every lord needs a seat.

Your **estate** is your anchor in Novus Mundus — the physical manifestation of everything you've built. It begins as little more than a claimed ruin: a collapsed foundation, a half-standing wall, a defensible position in a world that offers few.

Over time, it grows:

- **The Manor** — your hall, your home, your center of operations. Its size reflects your ambition. Its strength reflects your paranoia.
- **The Barracks** — where your guards train and your operatives plan. A lord without soldiers is a lord for exactly as long as it takes someone to notice.
- **The Warehouse** — where salvage is stored, sorted, and protected. In a relic economy, your warehouse is your treasury.
- **The Market** — where you trade with others. A thriving market attracts settlers, merchants, and — inevitably — the attention of rival Houses.

Each building is constructed from salvaged materials, reinforced with whatever the local ruins provide. No two estates look alike. Every one tells the story of the lord who built it and the ruin they built it on.

## X. The Threat That Lingers

The old scholars of Aeondral — the ones whose writings occasionally surface in sealed libraries deep in the ruins — believed the Novis veins were not a resource. They were a **system**. A circulatory network, like blood vessels in a body, carrying energy through the planet's crust.

Aeondral's mining operations didn't just deplete a resource. They **bled the world.**

The Sundering, in this interpretation, was not a random geological event. It was a **systemic failure** — the planet's structure collapsing because its internal support network was drained.

If this is true, then two questions haunt Novus Mundus:

1. **Are the veins refilling?** Some miners report finding Novis in places that were empty a year ago. Others say the crystals they find are different — warmer, more volatile, as if the energy is raw and unstable.

2. **If we drain them again, will it happen again?** Every NOVI crystal spent is a whisper of the old world's mistake. The survivors tell themselves it's different this time. They're being careful. They're using less.

They're telling themselves exactly what Aeondral told itself.

**No one knows when — or if — the next Sundering will come.** But the ground still trembles sometimes, in the deep places, where the old mines go down further than anyone is willing to follow.

### Regions of Novus Mundus

The 24 settlements are grouped into six regions — landmasses or archipelagos that emerged from the Sundering:

- **Ashenmere** — The central heartland. Temperate, ruin-dense, politically volatile. Where most survivors first gathered.
- **Duskfeld** — The cold north. Harsh winters preserve both ruins and grudges. Hard people, harder land.
- **Sunward Reach** — The arid south and east. Scorching heat, vast deserts, but beneath the sand: Novis.
- **Stormbreak Isles** — Eastern archipelago. Island kingdoms battered by storms, fiercely independent, hard to invade.
- **Jade Straits** — Tropical trade corridor. Dense jungle, humid, resource-rich. Everything grows here — including ambition.
- **Ironmarch** — The western continent. Vast, varied, still being explored. The newest settlements are here.
- **Greenvast** — The southern reaches. Lush highlands and deep mines. Rich in resources, poor in stability.

## XII. Narrative Hooks — Story in Play

The lore is not just backdrop. It surfaces during gameplay:

### Player Onboarding (Short Intro)
> *The old world fell. You don't remember it — no one alive does. What's left is ruin, rubble, and a world that doesn't care whether you survive.*
>
> *But you have. And now you're done just surviving.*
>
> *Claim your ground. Build your name. The world is being rewritten, and every lord who rises from the ashes gets to write a line.*
>
> *Welcome to Novus Mundus.*

### Encounter Flavor Text (Examples)
- **Goblin Raiders**: *"Scavengers. They were here before you, picking through the same ruins. They don't like competition."*
- **Troll Warlord**: *"It claimed this valley a generation ago. It remembers every trespass. It doesn't forgive."*
- **Wyvern**: *"The shadow passed overhead three times before it dove. By then it was too late to run."*
- **Dragon**: *"It watched your settlement for weeks. It wasn't hunting. It was deciding."*
- **Ancient Wyrm**: *"The miners broke through into a cavern that shouldn't exist. The cavern breathed."*

### Research Lore
As players unlock research nodes, they uncover fragments of Aeondral knowledge:
- Early research = practical survival knowledge ("how to reforge old-world steel")
- Mid research = understanding old-world systems ("the Novis conduit network")
- Late research = dangerous knowledge ("why the veins were never meant to be mined")

### Estate Lore
Each building upgrade reveals more about the ruin beneath:
- Manor Level 3: *"The foundation you're building on wasn't a house. It was a courthouse. The cells are still intact below."*
- Warehouse Level 5: *"Your workers broke into a sealed chamber. Inside: crates marked with a symbol no one recognizes. The contents are perfectly preserved."*

### Kingdom Launch Narrative
When a new kingdom becomes available:
> *"Sailors report new land visible on the eastern horizon — a landmass that wasn't there a month ago. The seabed has risen. The old-world ruins are already visible along the new coastline, half-crusted with coral and barnacle. The first ships are being prepared. The race begins."*

### Champions of Aeondral

Some salvage runs uncover more than stone. Deep in collapsed vaults, sealed beneath drowned cities, survivors occasionally find **tomb-crystals** — black-veined shards that hum when touched. These are not relics of any war Novus Mundus remembers. They are the bound memory-echoes of Aeondral's greatest figures: warriors, scholars, oracles, kings.

A survivor who cracks the seal does not summon a person. They wake a **remembrance** — bound to fight, build, or guide whoever broke the crystal. The names that surface from these echoes — Alexander, Caesar, Zeus, Cleopatra, Sun Wukong, Satoshi — are what survives in the song-fragments of Aeondral. Whether any of them lived as the stories tell is no longer knowable. The crystals are what's left.

The strongest sleep in the cities of their final stand. Most can be woken anywhere; some require a pilgrimage to the ruins where their tomb was sealed. The crystals are rare. The crystals are finite. And like NOVI, every one spent is a question: are we waking what should have stayed asleep?

## XIII. The Unwritten Rules of Novus Mundus

These are the thematic principles that should guide all narrative decisions:

1. **No chosen ones.** Every player is a survivor. Greatness is earned, not granted.
2. **The world is indifferent.** It is not evil. It is not good. It simply is. Survive it or don't.
3. **History repeats — maybe.** The Sundering happened because of greed and overreach. The survivors may be doing the same thing. The tension is intentional and unresolved.
4. **Power comes from people, not magic.** Houses rise because of alliances, strategy, and ruthlessness — not divine favor or mystical artifacts. NOVI enhances, but it doesn't choose sides.
5. **The real enemy is other players.** Creatures are obstacles. The weather is an obstacle. Other lords — they are the *threat*. Political intrigue is the heart of the game.
6. **The past is a resource, not a guide.** Aeondral fell. Its technology, its knowledge, its NOVI — all useful. Its example — a warning.
